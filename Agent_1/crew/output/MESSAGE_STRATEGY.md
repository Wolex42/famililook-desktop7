# Message Strategy: Character Mug Composition System
## Copywriter Agent | 30 March 2026 | Deliverable #2

---

## How to Read This Document

Every Character Mug tells a story. The composition engine picks a **scenario** based on the analysis data + context, and this document tells it exactly what to say, how loudly to say it, and in whose voice.

Each scenario section includes:
- **Headline pool** (5-10 options, all under 35 characters)
- **Tone description** — the emotional register
- **Score visibility** — prominent / subtle / hidden
- **Parent naming** — both parents, winner only, or neither
- **Speech bubble voice** — winner / child / narrator
- **Photo prominence** — large / small / hidden
- **Child name integration** — where the name sits in the layout

Character counts are noted in brackets. Newlines in headlines indicate the natural line break on the mug but are NOT counted toward the 35-character limit.

## 1. DECISIVE WIN (70%+ for one parent)

### Tone
Triumphant, playful, lightly competitive. The kind of teasing that happens at the dinner table. Never mean — always affectionate. The "losing" parent is the butt of a joke they would laugh at too.

### Score Visibility
**PROMINENT.** The high number IS the joke. "78% Mum" is the punchline that makes the headline land. Display in a bold gradient pill, large enough to read from across a table.

### Parent Naming
**Winner named in headline, loser named in subtext or speech bubble.** The winner owns the headline. The loser gets a playful mention ("Sorry Dad" energy).

### Speech Bubble Voice
**Winner's voice.** Proud, gloating, delighted. The winning parent is the narrator here.

### Photo Prominence
**Large.** The child's photo is proof. "Look at them — they really ARE all Mum." Photo anchors the claim.

### Child Name Integration
Name appears as a **standalone element** below the headline when provided. For decisive wins, the headline is about the parent ("MUMMY'S MINI ME"), and the child's name personalises beneath it: "OLIVIA | 78% Mum". If the name fits naturally, it can appear IN the headline (see H-DW-06 below).

## 2. MODERATE WIN (60-69%)

### Tone
Warm, proud, grounded. Less "we won the trophy" and more "the resemblance is real." Still celebratory, but the humour softens. This is about pride, not competition.

### Score Visibility
**SUBTLE.** Show the percentage, but in a smaller badge beneath the headline. The story is the bond, not the scoreboard. The number confirms; it does not dominate.

### Parent Naming
**Winner named warmly. Loser acknowledged positively.** "Mostly Mum, but Dad's in there too." The headline favours the winner; the feature callout often credits the loser with something specific.

### Speech Bubble Voice
**Narrator voice.** Gentle, observational. "The resemblance is real." Not gloating — noticing.

### Photo Prominence
**Large.** Same reasoning as decisive — the photo proves the claim. But the photo should feel more "family portrait" than "evidence."

### Child Name Integration
Name works well **in the headline** at this tier. "MOSTLY MUM, {child}" or as a standalone element. The moderate win is personal, so the name adds warmth.

## 3. CLOSE CALL (51-59%)

### Tone
Celebratory unity. "Best of both" energy. The close call is NOT a competition — it is a celebration of the blend. The tone is warm, wonder-filled, and inclusive. Both parents contributed. The child is the masterpiece of the collaboration.

### Score Visibility
**HIDDEN or very subtle.** At 51-54%, showing "53% Mum" undermines the blend message. If shown at all, it should be tiny text, almost a footnote. At 55-59%, it can be subtle (small badge). Never prominent.

### Parent Naming
**BOTH parents named.** "A bit of Mum, a bit of Dad." Neither parent "won" in the emotional framing — they both contributed. Headlines that name both parents feel inclusive and warm.

### Speech Bubble Voice
**Child's voice** (if speech bubble is used). "I GOT THE BEST BITS!" or "BEST OF BOTH!" The child is the narrator of their own blend. Alternatively, use no speech bubble — let the headline carry the message.

### Photo Prominence
**Large, central.** The child is the star. In a close call, the child IS the story — the unique mix that neither parent could have produced alone.

### Child Name Integration
Name goes **IN the headline** whenever possible. "THE BEST OF BOTH, {child}" or as a standalone hero element. The close call is the most child-centric scenario.

## 4. GIFT FOR WINNER PARENT

_Mum won the analysis. The mug is a gift FOR Mum._

### Tone
Direct address. Proud, personal, "this is YOURS." The mug speaks to the recipient. It is not a report — it is a love letter from the data. "Your mini me" hits differently from "Mummy's mini me" when you are the mum holding the mug.

### Score Visibility
**PROMINENT.** The recipient will love seeing the number. "YOUR mini me — 78% you" is a badge of honour. Show it proudly.

### Parent Naming
**Second person ("YOUR") replaces the parent label.** "YOUR MINI ME" instead of "MUMMY'S MINI ME." The loser parent can appear in a speech bubble or subtext as playful contrast.

### Speech Bubble Voice
**Child's voice.** "I LOOK LIKE YOU!" or "THANKS FOR THE GENES!" The child speaks TO the parent. This makes the gift feel like it comes from the child.

### Photo Prominence
**Large.** The parent is holding a mug with their child's face on it. The photo is the emotional core.

### Child Name Integration
Name as a **standalone element** between headline and score. "YOUR MINI ME" / "OLIVIA" / "78% You" — three-line stack that reads as a complete gift message.

## 5. GIFT FOR LOSING PARENT — CRITICAL SCENARIO

_Mum won the analysis. But the mug is a gift FOR DAD._

This is the most emotionally sensitive scenario in the entire system. If handled badly, the mug says "Your child doesn't look like you" — a terrible gift. If handled well, the mug celebrates what Dad DID contribute, wraps him into the family story, and makes him feel seen.

### Strategy: Find the Feature, Celebrate the Bond

The composition engine MUST:
1. Look at the `featureVotes` and find which features belong to the losing parent
2. Lead with THAT feature as the hero message
3. Frame the overall result as a family celebration, not a competition Dad lost

If Dad has 3 of 8 features (e.g., smile, hair, ears), the mug should say "THEY'VE GOT YOUR SMILE" — not "Sorry, Mum won."

### Tone
Warm, inclusive, affirming. Slightly conspiratorial — "between you and me, they got the best bit from you." The tone says: "You are essential to this child. The data proves it."

### Score Visibility
**HIDDEN.** Do not show "78% Mum" on a mug that is a gift for Dad. The score tells the wrong story for this recipient. If any number is shown, it should be the feature-specific one: "3 features from Dad" or nothing.

### Parent Naming
**Recipient (the "loser") is the star.** Dad is addressed directly. Mum may appear in a gentle secondary mention or not at all. The mug is about Dad and the child.

### Speech Bubble Voice
**Child's voice.** "I GOT YOUR SMILE!" or "THAT'S FROM YOU, DAD!" The child speaks to Dad about the specific feature they share. This is the heart of the gift.

### Photo Prominence
**Large.** The child's photo anchors the message. Dad sees his child and the message about their shared feature.

### Child Name Integration
Name goes **in the headline or as a standalone element.** "{child} GOT YOUR SMILE" is deeply personal.

## 6. GIFT FOR GRANDPARENT

### Tone
Warm, proud, showing-off energy. Grandparents want to display their grandchild to the world. The mug should feel like a brag — "Look at my grandchild. They are wonderful. And here is the proof." Slightly formal (not cheeky), deeply warm.

### Score Visibility
**SUBTLE or HIDDEN.** Grandparents care less about which parent won and more about the grandchild being celebrated. If the winner IS the grandparent's own child (e.g., Gran is Mum's mum and Mum won), the score can be subtle. Otherwise, hide it.

### Parent Naming
**Grandparent is addressed. Parents may be referenced.** "Your grandchild looks like your [son/daughter]" is powerful for grandparents — it connects the generations. But if the winner is the OTHER parent (Gran is Mum's mum but Dad won), lead with the child, not the score.

### Speech Bubble Voice
**Child's voice or narrator.** "LOOK AT ME, GRAN!" or "Officially adorable — confirmed by AI." Warm, not competitive.

### Photo Prominence
**LARGE.** This is the selling point for grandparents. They want to see their grandchild's face every morning with their tea.

### Child Name Integration
Name goes **IN the headline.** Grandparents mugs are the most name-centric. "{child}: OFFICIALLY ADORABLE" is the template.

## 8. CHILD NAME INTEGRATION RULES

The child's name is the single most powerful personalisation element. A mug that says "OLIVIA: MUM'S MINI ME" is a keepsake. A mug that says "MUMMY'S MINI ME" is a product. The difference is the name.

### Rule 1: When to Put the Name IN the Headline

- **Birthday occasion**: ALWAYS. The birthday is the child's event.
- **Grandparent gift**: ALWAYS. Gran wants to see the name.
- **Close call / blend**: PREFERRED. "{child}: BEST OF BOTH" centres the child.
- **Gift for losing parent**: PREFERRED. "{child} GOT YOUR SMILE" makes it personal.

### Rule 2: When to Use the Name as a Standalone Element

- **Decisive win**: The headline celebrates the parent. The name sits below as a standalone: "MUMMY'S MINI ME" / [OLIVIA] / "78% Mum"
- **Gift for winner parent**: "YOUR MINI ME" / [OLIVIA] / "78% You"
- **Any scenario where the headline is already 25+ characters**: Adding the name would break the 35-char limit.

### Rule 3: When to Put the Name in the Score Line

- **Moderate win**: "{child}: 64% Mum" — name in the score line ties the data to the person.
- This is a fallback when the name is not in the headline or standalone.

### Rule 4: Name Absent

When `childName` is not provided:
- Remove the standalone name element entirely
- Do not use "{child}" templates — fall back to non-name variants
- The layout engine should reclaim the space (tighten vertical gaps)
- Never show a blank or placeholder where the name would be

### Rule 5: Name Length Limits

- Names under 8 characters: can appear in headlines freely
- Names 8-12 characters: use standalone element (headline space is tight)
- Names 13+ characters: truncate with a nickname prompt ("What do you call them?") or use first name only
- ALL names render in ALL CAPS on the mug to match the headline typography

## 10. SPEECH BUBBLE STRATEGY

Speech bubbles add character and humour. They are the voice of the cartoon character on the mug. But they must be used carefully — too many words on a mug creates noise.

### When to Use Speech Bubbles

| Scenario | Use bubble? | Voice | Tone |
|----------|------------|-------|------|
| Decisive win | YES | Winner parent | Proud, gloating: "TOLD YOU SO!" |
| Moderate win | OPTIONAL | Narrator | Observational: "THE RESEMBLANCE IS REAL" |
| Close call | OPTIONAL | Child | Playful: "I GOT THE BEST BITS!" |
| Gift for losing parent | NO | — | The bubble risks adding a competitive note to a sensitive gift. |
| Gift for winner parent | YES | Child | Affectionate: "I LOOK LIKE YOU!" |
| Grandparent | OPTIONAL | Child | Cute: "LOOK AT ME, GRAN!" |
| Mother's Day | OPTIONAL | Child | Warm: "LOVE YOU, MUM" |
| Father's Day | OPTIONAL | Child | Fun: "YOU'RE THE BEST, DAD" |
| Birthday | NO | — | Too many text elements already (occasion + name + headline). |
| Christmas | OPTIONAL | Narrator | Festive: "SEASON'S GREETINGS!" |

### Speech Bubble Copy Banks

**Winner proud (decisive/moderate win):**

## 11. SECOND CHARACTER STRATEGY

The "losing" parent character (e.g., Papa Bear looking surprised while Mama Bear celebrates) adds humour and visual balance. But it must be used thoughtfully.

### When to Include a Second Character

| Scenario | Second character? | Their emotion | Reasoning |
|----------|------------------|---------------|-----------|
| Decisive win (70%+) | YES | Surprised / mock-offended | The rivalry is the joke. Both characters sell it. |
| Moderate win (60-69%) | OPTIONAL | Slightly impressed | Less rivalry, more acknowledgement. |
| Close call (51-59%) | YES | Both celebrating equally | Two characters flanking = unity layout. |
| Gift for losing parent | NO | — | The mug is FOR them. Don't show them "losing." |
| Gift for winner parent | NO | — | Keep focus on the recipient + child. |
| Grandparent | NO | — | Gran/Gramps is the character. Parents are not shown. |
| Mother's Day | NO (unless playful tone) | — | Keep focus on Mum + child. |
| Father's Day | OPTIONAL | Dad celebrating | Father's Day can handle more playful rivalry. |

## 12. COMPOSITION PRIORITY MATRIX

When the layout has room for only N elements, this is the priority order for each scenario.

### Universal Minimum (always present, 3 elements)
1. Character illustration
2. Hero headline
3. Brand mark (tiny)

### Priority by Scenario (elements 4-7)

**Decisive win (Layout A: Hero):**
4. Score badge (prominent)
5. Child name (standalone)
6. Feature callout
7. Speech bubble OR second character (not both)

**Moderate win (Layout A or B):**
4. Child name (standalone or in headline)
5. Score badge (subtle)
6. Feature callout
7. Photo (if available)

**Close call (Layout C: Blend):**
4. Child name (in headline)
5. Photo (prominent)
6. Second character (both parents)
7. Score badge (subtle/hidden)

**Gift for losing parent (Layout B: Celebration):**
4. Child name (in headline or standalone)
5. Photo (large)
6. Feature callout (CRITICAL — the feature they contributed)
7. Score: HIDDEN

**Gift for winner parent (Layout B: Celebration):**
4. Child name (standalone)
5. Photo (large)
6. Score badge (prominent — it's a compliment)
7. Speech bubble (child's voice)

**Grandparent (Layout D: Gift):**
4. Child name (in headline)
5. Photo (large — this is the selling point)
6. Occasion tag (if applicable)
7. Generational feature callout (if applicable)

**Any occasion (Layout D: Gift):**
4. Occasion header (always, top position)
5. Child name (in headline or header)
6. Photo (large)
7. Feature callout OR speech bubble (not both — space is tight)

## 13. BRAND VOICE GUARDRAILS

### Always
- Warm, playful, celebratory
- Affectionate rivalry (never mean-spirited)
- Family-positive (all family structures welcome)
- "AI-powered" or "for entertainment" disclaimer in marketing (not on the mug itself)

### Never
- Clinical or scientific language ("DNA," "genetic," "hereditary traits")
- Negative framing of the losing parent ("Dad lost," "Mum barely contributed")
- 50/50 — always show a nudge, always pick a story
- Language targeting children under 13
- Health or ancestry claims
- Copy that implies single parents are "missing" something

### Tone Test
Before any headline ships, ask: "If I were the losing parent and someone gave me this mug, would I laugh or wince?" If the answer is wince, rewrite.

## 14. CUSTOM PARENT NAMES

Not everyone uses "Mum" and "Dad." The system supports custom names (e.g., "Baba," "Amma," "Papa," "Nana"). When the parent label is not in the MUM_ALIASES or DAD_ALIASES list, the engine uses the `custom` headline pool.

### Custom Name Headlines

| ID | Headline | Chars* |
|----|----------|--------|
| H-CU-01 | {winner}'S MINI ME | 17* |
| H-CU-02 | ALL {winner} | 11* |
| H-CU-03 | {child} LOOKS LIKE {winner} | 27* |
| H-CU-04 | MADE BY {winner} | 15* |
| H-CU-05 | {winner}'S GREATEST HIT | 22* |
| H-CU-06 | {winner}'S DOUBLE | 16* |
| H-CU-07 | {winner} WINS | 11* |
| H-CU-08 | HELLO, MINI {winner} | 19* |

_*Character counts assume a 5-character custom name. The 35-char limit is enforced at render time._

### Custom Name Rules
- Custom names render in ALL CAPS to match headline typography
- If the custom name + headline exceeds 35 characters, fall back to a shorter headline variant
- Never assume gender from a custom name — use neutral phrasing where possible
